One-variable calculus in multiple dimensions is not particularly terrifying. From Linear Algebra, we already know that a function from \(\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^n\) will have potentially large column space and potentially no null space at all, so there is really only one dimension of information we need to work with.

It may be beneficial to treat multi-dimensional real numbers as vectors, or tuples of numbers.

Norm and properties of numbers in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

Requires: \nameref{number-theory}, \nameref{vectors}

We need a property of distance in multi-dimensional space, or a vector space. The one we shall use for this section is the standard Euclidean norm, which is a generalisation of the one-dimensional absolute value. We denote the norm of a vector as such: \[\forall (x_1, \dots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n \quad \norm{(x_1, \dots, x_n)} := \left(\sum_{j=1}^n x_j^2\right)^{1/2} \]

That is to say, the norm of a vector is defined as the square root of the sum the squares of its individual components.

The important thing to note is that the norm has the property that whenever two points are together in multi-dimensional space (their coordinates are close to each other), then we have that the norm of their difference will be small.

Recall \nameref{continuity}. A function \(f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^n\) has continuity defined similarly, since we only have to track one input variable. It is continuous at point \(t \in \mathbb{R}\) if:

\[ \forall \epsilon > 0,\ \exists \delta > 0,\ |h| < \delta \Rightarrow \norm{f(t+h) - f(t)} < \epsilon \]

That is to say, for any error (\(\epsilon\)), there is a difference such that whenever we shift the input of the function by an amount (\(h\)) smaller than that distance (\(\delta\)), our function error will be smaller. We can always move closer and closer to our point, with the error decreasing.

Definition

Lv1

\(f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^n\) is differentiable at \(t \in \mathbb{R}\) if: \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(t+h) - f(t)}{h} \] exists.

Lv2

In one dimension, our function can move either up or down, and our derivative tells us how fast we're going up or down. In two dimensions, we can go vertically or horizontally, and our derivative should tell us how fast we're going in those two directions.

In \(n\) dimensions, our derivative will tell us how fast we're going in those \(n\) directions, and since every direction is the output of one component function, our derivative of the entire function is the derivative of every component function as a vector.

TODO Integral Calculus on a Curve

Lv1

Another way of looking at a functions \(\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^n\) is to view them as functions that draw lines, or curves, in multi-dimensional space.

Let's call our curve \(\C\).

\[ \C = \{ f(t) | t \in [a, b] \} \]

This means that our curve \(\mathcal{C}\) is described by the set of all points that are the result of the function \(f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^n\) over the interval \([a, b]\).

The length of this curve is: \[ \abs{\C} := \int_a^b \norm{f'(t)} dt \]

Lv2

Our integral on the curve will no longer be about area, but about length.

Just as the Riemann integral approximated the area underneath a curve by summing up many little rectangles, the idea behind integration on a curve is to find the length of the curve by drawing many small line segments.

If we add enough of them together, we can estimate the length of the curve through an integral. But which integral?

Lv3

Suppose our 'curve' is actually just a line:

\[ \C = \{ X + t(Y - X) | t \in [0, 1] \} \quad X, Y \in \mathbb{R}^n \]

So our line is between \(X\) and \(Y\). In that case the length is just the norm of the distance:

\[ \abs{\C} = \norm{X - Y} = \left(\sum_{j=1}^n (x_j - y_j)^2\right)^{1/2} \]
